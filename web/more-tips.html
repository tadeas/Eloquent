Speaking tips
Research the audience
Quoting Guy Kawasaki, "If you can prove to your audience in the first five minutes that you understand who they are, you've got them for the rest of the speech." Understand the issues, competition and trends that the audience faces. You can speak to the organizer of the meeting and ask about the audience and their expectations as well as their demographics, age, background, gender, etc. If you present at an industry event, research the event web site and do a Google search about the industry in general. If you are presenting to a corporation, learn as much as you can about that corporation and what challenges it faces.
Get familiar with the room
If it's possible, visit the talk location prior to the speaking event. See how the room will be laid out, make any requests for positioning any visual aid equipment. Check how big the room is and if you need a microphone. When you know what's ahead of you, you can prepare better. And what's most important, you'll be less anxious and more confident.
Talk to the audience before you start
Greet the audience at the door. Ask questions to learn about their expectations and level of knowledge. You are here to fulfill their expectations after all and delivering that will make a huge difference in how they perceive you. You'll also make a few friends and it always helps to have friends in the room.
Exercise a bit before speaking
Exercising lightly before a presentation can get your blood circulating and send more oxygen to your brain. Take a walk before the speech or do a few squats or something similar.
Don't take audience too personally
Have you ever been to an interesting speech, but were too tired to really pay attention? There is always going to be someone in the audience on their phone or yawning. Remember that there will always be people who are bored or tired. It's their problem, not yours. None of these audience reactions have anything to do with you personally.
Practice in front of a mirror
Recording and reviewing impromptu speech helps tremendously. Watching yourself in the mirror a couple of times helps too. Practice the speech as if you were speaking directly to someone. Focus on your facial expressions, gestures, body movements, how welcoming you appear in general. Gentle expressions and calm demeanor make you appear more welcoming.
Talk about something your audience is interested in
Sounds like a no brainer. But many otherwise intelligent people talk about what they are interested in, rather than what the audience is interested in. When speaking at a conference, people come to your session because of the synopsis. That's what they're interested in. Don't change it just because it suits you. Circulating with the audience before the talk helps understanding the audience interests better.
Tell them why they should listen
So the audience is interested in the topic you are going to speak about. Great. Unfortunately, it's not always that way. In either, case it's good to sparkle even more interest. How will the speech benefit them? Why should they care? If you're speaking about health and safety, you can tell them about a coworker who got injured in the workplace and what consequences it had for him.
Audience of two or two thousand?
Everybody can deliver a great speech to an audience of two people. How is it different from giving a speech to two thousand people? Now it's a very public speech and we focus on the public part, instead of the speaking part. To be an effective speaker, you must do the opposite, focus on the speaking and let go the public. With two people listening we have no problems being ourselves. Just be yourself in front of two thousands too.
Don't fuss about mistakes
Even the most experienced speakers make mistakes. How do we know? To err is human, so there simply must be some mistakes, even in the most perfect speeches. But we don't notice. We don't know the speaker left out a word, said a wrong name or skipped a page. Never apologize for a minor slip. Unless the mistake was really earth shattering, they didn't notice anyway, so don't tell them.
Make it personal
People like to hear about other people's experiences. Their triumphs, tragedies and everyday humorous anecdotes, anything the audience can identify with. Whenever possible, insert a personal element in the speech. Tell stories. Taking inspiration from sales and marketing, use "customer personas" to give examples.
Entertain
The primary goal of the speech is to entertain the audience. If people enjoy it, you can slip in some information. But if the speech is utterly dull, no amount of information will make it great. If we could pick between an entertaining and informative speech, most people would choose the entertaining one. Some informing can happen too.
Change grabs attention
We notice change. You notice the hum of the air-conditioner when it comes on and when it goes off - but not in between. You can use this natural human propensity to retrieve your audience's attention. Switch from slides to flipchart and back again. Show a short video. Change speaking style according to the content. When making statements of fact, use a measured, deliberate tone and stand still. When telling a story, speed up and move around.
Avoid cliches
Oh God, please save us from... In spite of his busy schedule... Sparing a few precious moments of his time... When the organizers came to invite me I was... Unless and Until... None other than... Last but not the least... Friend, philosopher and guide... I am sorry to take up so much of your time... With these words I conclude my speech... For making this function a grand success... Thought provoking speech... I assure the speaker... A token of our love...
Repeat audience questions
Unless microphones are available, rarely will everyone in the audience hear questions other audience members ask. Always repeat the question and then answer it. It's not only courteous, but it also provides you with a little more time to think of an awesome way to answer each question. And you'll make sure that you understood the question right too.
